Case Study

Documented real-world incidents that reveal how AI misinformation operates in legal, political, and social contexts.

Mata v. Avianca Airlinesclick

In the "Mata v. Avianca Airlines" case, lawyer Steven A. Schwartz, when handling aviation injury disputes, used ChatGPT to assist in generating defense pleadings in order to increase the efficiency of document writing. However, he did not conduct manual verification of the legal basis provided by the AI. Eventually, in the documents submitted to the court, he cited six completely fabricated precedents — the case numbers, the courts where the cases were heard, and the names of the judges were all fabricated by ChatGPT.

Even when Schwartz questioned the authenticity of the precedents, the AI clearly claimed that they were "absolutely true" (U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York, 2023).[17]

This case clearly reveals the core misunderstanding of the public regarding AI: believing that the information generated by AI is authoritative and true, and can be directly used in professional scenarios. The truth is, the core function of current large models is only to fit language patterns and predict reasonable expressions, and they do not have the ability to verify facts. The "hallucinations" produced by them are essentially the result of optimizing language fluency rather than factual authenticity by the model.

AI Creates Biden's Voice to Call Votersclick

During the 2024 US presidential election, 5,000 Democratic voters in New Hampshire received automated voice calls. The voice on the call was highly similar to that of President Biden, even imitating his catchphrase "That's complete nonsense". The call misled the voters by telling them not to vote in the state primary, claiming that participating in the primary would prevent them from voting in the November general election.

After investigation, the voice was commissioned by a senior Democratic political advisor, Steve Kramer, and was created using AI voice cloning software from "Eleven Labs", which completed the deepfake of Biden's voice in less than 30 minutes. The incident triggered a criminal investigation and the US Federal Communications Commission plans to classify AI automated voice calls as illegal.

This case clearly reveals that Deepfake technology can clone the voices and tones of public figures, generating highly realistic false speech content. The public is prone to fall into misconceptions of cognition due to the "realistic voice", ignoring the possibility of AI forgery. Deepfake is essentially a synthesis technology based on generative adversarial networks, with the core being to restore the voices and facial features of public figures, rather than transmitting true information. Such forgery acts not only mislead the public and disrupt order, but also may cross legal red lines.
